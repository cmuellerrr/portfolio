Summary

Every NASA flight goes through a series of reviews to certify that it is safe to launch. These reviews typically involve lots of documentation and presentations discussing whether or not there are potential issues. This is typically a very long and laborious process, so we developed software to help streamline the way data is gathered and presented at certification reviews for future Exploration missions.

Keep reading for more details on our process

The challenge

In order to make sure that every mission is safe to launch, NASA has what's known as a Certification of Flight Readiness (CoFR) process. The CoFR process is a series of milestone reviews where the organizations responsible for the many parts of a mission present their status and certify whether or not they have met their requirements for that milestone. The board members of that review then determine if the mission is fit to continue to the next milestone. This eventually leads up to the day of launch, where a final "go" or "no-go" decision is made1.

This is an image from the Flight Readiness Review (FRR) for STS-121. Image courtesy of NASA.

This involves manually gathering mounds of data from lots of different sources, analyzing said data, then documenting that analysis in a presentation.

We were asked to create an electronic system that assists the collection, relationship, and presentation of CoFR data to increase time available for analysis and closure activities.

Our process

We started our research with a literature review of Space Shuttle Program (SSP) CoFR reviews. We also observed a current CoFR review for the International Space Station (ISS). Through an artifact analysis of Exploration Systems Development (ESD) CoFR plans and retrospective interviews with people with past CoFR experience, we were able to make a model of the CoFR process for upcoming ESD missions.

With this model, we began our process of developing role-based personas. We use role-based personas to understand the types of users we'll have, and their differing needs. We then used these personas to create a set of user stories that laid out all of the goals we need to address is our design.

As a <type of user>, I want <some goal> so that <some reason>.

Since this project had a lot of visibility and reached broadly across NASA, we knew that people would need to see beyond wireframes and storyboards in order to buy in to the vision. So, we built a full proof of concept from scratch to display the overall design direction. This put us on a whirlwind tour of demos and discussions, but ended up being vital to conveying the concept to stakeholders.

Our prototype let us quickly explore different ideas for modeling the complicated data structure.

Our solution

There are two primary components of the CoFR process that we are designing for: data analysis and review facilitation.

Firstly, we focused on the general management of a CoFR review; make sure that the basics — like signing endorsements and indicating an exception to a requirement — are simple and straightforward. Inefficiencies in the primary review processes will negate any efficiencies we gain elsewhere. Our system is designed to provide a simple interface for creating and linking data items, digitally signing documents, and reporting on data.

The system also provides real-time metrics to more easily understand the massive data set.

Secondly, we wanted to look at reducing the amount of time organizations spend gathering and tracking data from various external systems. NASA has a countless number of systems that contain various sorts of data and the only way for an organization to get a full picture of their status is to look at all those systems, one by one2. Our design is aiming to provide a centralized portal where users can query external systems and track data that affects their flight readiness â€” no more spreadsheets.

Currently, engineers need to track work that exists in each system individually.
